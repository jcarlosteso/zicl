What is LISP?

If I just said LISP is a programming language, most of you would figuratively walk away from this site, never to look back. But, yes, at its heart of hearts, LISP is yet another way humans have found to represent algorithms.

And it’s one of the first programming languages, too. The second, after Fortran. And, like Fortran, it was conceived as a way to represent mathematical expressions.

But LISP is nothing like Fortran. While the latter is an imperative language, the former is a functional one. In the following entries, we will see what does that exactly mean.

The basic element of every LISP dialect is called the form, which actually means “anything that can be evaluated.” There’s only two types of forms: atoms, and cons.

Atoms are simple values. They can be self-evaluated —like 1, which is a simple number without a particular name attached to it— or symbols —which refer indirectly to a value.

Cons, on the other hand, are pairs of things. The first of these things (called the CAR) is always an atom. The second (called the CDR) can be an atom, too, but it can also be a reference to a next cons.

A list, then, is nothing more than a chain of cons. A list properly built will have a nil —or an empty list— as its CDR.
